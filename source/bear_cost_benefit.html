1. Encounter rate λ (encounters per year)
Rate of bear encounters per year on this property. I_y is the estimated number of bear incursions per year per confident bear in the surrounding area; N_bears is the number of habituated/confident bears in the area; the food-availability modifier tunes the rate. Damage probabilities P(D_i|B,E) are no longer averaged into λ — they are applied per element in step 3.
λ = I_y · N_bears · food

2. Element-level encounter rate
The total encounter rate is split across the three element types according to the encounter share s_enc_i, which represents the fraction of bear visits targeting each element type.
λ_i = λ · s_enc_i

3. Annual loss without protection
Each encounter targeting element i causes damage with probability P(D_i|B,E), and a damaging event takes u_i units. Expected units damaged per year is therefore P(D_i|B,E) · λ_i · u_i, capped by the available stock N_i — you can't lose more than you have.
loss_i^noprot = min( P(D_i|B,E) · λ_i · u_i , N_i ) · c_i

4. Annual cost with protection (covering a share s_prot_i of N_i units)
Protection splits the stock into N_unprot = (1 − s_prot_i) · N_i unprotected units and N_prot = s_prot_i · N_i protected units. The bear's damaging "appetite" P(D_i|B,E) · λ_i · u_i is split between the two pools in proportion to their share of stock: fraction (1 − s_prot_i) of attempts target unprotected units and always succeed; fraction s_prot_i target protected units and succeed only when the measure has failed (P(¬M_j)). Each pool is then capped by its own available stock. At s_prot_i = 0 the unprotected term reduces to the no-protection loss in step 3.

Losses from unprotected units — appetite share (1 − s_prot_i) hits this pool, capped by N_unprot.
unprot_i = min( P(D_i|B,E) · λ_i · u_i · (1 − s_prot_i) , N_unprot ) · c_i

Losses from non-maintained infrastructure (measure fails) — appetite share s_prot_i reaches the protected pool but only succeeds with probability P(¬M_j); capped by N_prot.
residual_i = min( P(D_i|B,E) · λ_i · u_i · s_prot_i · P(¬M_j) , N_prot ) · c_i

Maintenance per year — recurring annual cost of keeping each protected unit functional. The per-unit maintenance rate is element-specific.
maint_i/yr = c_maintenance_i · N_i · s_prot_i

Installation (per cycle) — paid upfront when the measure is deployed, and re-paid every lifespan_j years when the measure reaches end-of-life and is replaced.
install_i = c_install_i · N_i · s_prot_i

5. Cumulative cost over T years
Without protection, cumulative cost grows linearly: T years of compensation. With protection, installation is paid in year 0 and re-paid every lifespan_j years (the chart shows step jumps at each re-investment). Each year between installations adds maintenance plus residual plus unprotected exposure. The crossover year — when the protected line drops below the unprotected one — is the payback period.
C_noprot(T) = T · Σ loss_i^noprot
C_prot(T) = (1 + ⌊T / lifespan_j⌋) · Σ install_i + T · Σ (unprot_i + residual_i + maint_i/yr)

Net benefit at T
Net(T) = C_noprot(T) − C_prot(T)

Model overview

"Ursus" (latin for bear) is an interactive tool to perform a cost-benefit analysis of preventive measures for bear-caused damage in the Italian Apennines region, particularly in the area of influence of the NGO Salviamo l'orso.

The tool always compares two different scenarios: no protective measures implemented and protective measures are implemented. Both scenarios assume the compensation policy currently in place, in which residents and farmers can claim monetary compensation if they have suffered property loss or damage due to a bear.

In the no protection scenario all the costs go to compensation payed to the owner for all damage ocurred, without any protective infrastructe.

In the protection implemented scenario funds are used to install and maintain protective measures or bear proof infrastructure (electric fences, bear-proof barns, etc) covering a chosen share of each asset type. Even with measures, some damage still occurs, so compensation is also calculated.

The model tracks three asset types — small livestock (sheep, goats, poultry), large livestock (cattle, horses), and agriculture (beehives, orchards). Each of them has differentiated characteristics (costs, rates of encounter, probability of damage, etc.). The key output is the cumulative cost curve for both strategies.

Bear encounter pressure is driven by the number of bears habituated to human infrastructure ("confident bears") and a baseline incursion rate, defined based on historical data.

How to use the tool

The tool is based on default values that were sourced through communication with experts and official data from Salviamo l'Orso and Parco Nazionale d'Abruzzo. This values are shown in the "Data Source" page.

For ease of use, we have created two different viewing modes of the model. The complete view includes sliders for all variable that are evaluated. In that sense, it is more complex to use but also offers more precise scenarios.

The simple view has a reduced number of sliders, showing most relevant variables and reducing the complexity of the tool. This version is intended for easy scenario making and for engaging with non-expert stakeholders

All views are connected, and the model behind each is shared. A change made on the Simple View page is immediately reflected on the Complete View page and in the Data Source table, and vice versa.

The Generate Scenarios page has a set of predefined analytical scenarios that you can use to understand how different variables affect the cost-benefit ratio of the measures. You can use either the simple or complete view pages to play with the variables and define your own scenarios.

Understanding the sections

General context panel: Here you can set the time horizon, bear pressure, protective measure effectiveness and lifespan. These apply to all asset types.

Per-asset panels: Use it to tune parameters for each asset type individually. This includes total amount of units, what share is protected and compensation, installation and maintenance costs of the measure.

Result cards: Define each asset type shows an annual cost breakdown and a cumulative cost chart. The overall chart aggregates all three types and displays the net benefit of protection at the chosen horizon.

Reset button: Restore all sliders to the default values.

The verdict in the page header updates in real time and summarises whether protection or compensation is the better strategy at the chosen time horizon.

Data source

The database contains 31 variables organised into groups: bear pressure, environment modifiers, per-element damage probabilities, encounter shares, maintenance probability, unit counts, units damaged per event, compensation costs, protection coverage, installation and maintenance costs, measure lifespan, and analysis time horizon.

Two variables are derived and cannot be edited directly. Agriculture encounter share (s_enc_Ag) is automatically computed as the residual of livestock shares (1 − s_enc_SL − s_enc_LL) so that encounter shares always sum to 100%. Measure failure probability (P(¬M_j)) is the residual of maintained units (1 − P(M_j)).

To change the default values permanently, edit the Excel workbook and regenerate the HTML by running python source/pipeline.py.
